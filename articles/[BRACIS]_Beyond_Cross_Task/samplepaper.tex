\begin{abstract}
Multi-task learning for point-of-interest prediction typically pairs joint
next-category and next-region heads over a shared point-of-interest graph
embedding, with the implicit assumption that joint training transfers signal
between tasks. We replace the per-place substrate with a check-in-level
contextual graph embedding that emits one vector per visit and run a
controlled cross-state ablation on Gowalla under user-disjoint cross-validation
with leak-free per-fold transition priors. The substrate alone lifts
single-task next-category macro-F1 by 15 percentage points at smaller states
and 29 to 33 points at the headline scale, head-invariant. With the substrate
fixed, joint multi-task training over a cross-attention backbone gains a small
additional category lift at every state and pays a sign-consistent
next-region cost---but the cost shrinks as data scales, evidence that
multi-task learning begins to generalise on the harder task with more data.
Drop-in balancers and head-capacity fixes do not recover the gap.

\keywords{Multi-Task Learning \and Point-of-Interest Prediction \and Graph
Embeddings.}
\end{abstract}
